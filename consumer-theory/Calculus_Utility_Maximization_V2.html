Step 1: Setup & The Budget Constraint

The consumer's problem: Choose quantities of good X and good Y to maximize utility, subject to a budget constraint.

Budget Constraint:
Px × Qx + Py × Qy = Income

With current values:

$2.00 × Qx + $3.00 × Qy = $60
X-intercept (spend all on X): Qx = 30.0  |  Y-intercept (spend all on Y): Qy = 20.0
Key Concept — The Budget Line: The red line on the graph represents all affordable bundles. The slope equals −Px/Py = −0.67. This is the rate at which the market trades X for Y.

Step 2: The Cobb-Douglas Utility Function

We work with a specific utility function that has clean mathematical properties:

U = Qxα × Qy(1−α)

With current α = 0.60:

U = Qx0.60 × Qy0.40
What does α tell us?
  • α = 0.60 means the consumer has a relative preference for good X
  • The consumer will spend 60% of income on X
  • The consumer will spend 40% of income on Y
  • This spending split is independent of prices — only α matters!
Try it: Change α with the slider. Notice how the spending percentages in the Optimal Solution box update immediately — and the graph shows the optimal bundle shift.
Why Cobb-Douglas?
  • Generates smooth, convex indifference curves (the teal curves on the graph)
  • Has diminishing marginal utility in each good
  • Produces clean, closed-form solutions (no messy algebra)
  • Widely used in economics and finance

Step 3: Computing Marginal Utilities

Marginal utility measures the extra utility from consuming one more unit. We find these by taking partial derivatives of the utility function.

Marginal Utility of X:
MUx = ∂U/∂Qx = α × Qx(α−1) × Qy(1−α)

With α = 0.60:

MUx = 0.60 × Qx−0.40 × Qy0.40

Note: MUx decreases as Qx rises (since the exponent on Qx is negative) — this is diminishing marginal utility.

Marginal Utility of Y:
MUy = ∂U/∂Qy = (1−α) × Qxα × Qy(−α)
MUy = 0.40 × Qx0.60 × Qy−0.60
Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS):
MRS = MUx / MUy = (α / (1−α)) × (Qy / Qx)

With current α: MRS = 1.50 × (Qy / Qx)

The MRS is the slope of the indifference curve: how many units of Y the consumer would give up for one more X.

Step 4: The Optimization Condition

The golden rule of consumer optimization: At the optimal bundle, the last dollar spent on each good delivers the same marginal utility.

MUx / Px = MUy / Py
Equivalently — MRS equals the price ratio:
MRS = Px / Py

The slope of the indifference curve equals the slope of the budget line. This is the tangency condition you see on the graph.

Substituting our MRS expression:
(α / (1−α)) × (Qy / Qx) = Px / Py
After rearranging:
α / (1−α) = (Px × Qx) / (Py × Qy)

With current values: α/(1−α) = 1.50

This means: Spending on X / Spending on Y = 1.50

Verification with current values: Spending on X = $36.00  |  Spending on Y = $24.00
Ratio = 1.50 ✓ (matches α/(1−α))

Step 5: Solving for Optimal Quantities

We have two equations and two unknowns (Qx and Qy):

Equation 1 — Optimization condition (from Step 4):
Px × Qx = [α / (1−α)] × Py × Qy
Equation 2 — Budget constraint:
Px × Qx + Py × Qy = I
  1. Substitute Eq. 1 into Eq. 2:   [α/(1−α)] PyQy + PyQy = I
  2. Factor out PyQy:   PyQy [α/(1−α) + 1] = I
  3. Simplify bracket:   PyQy [1/(1−α)] = I
  4. Solve for Qy:   Qy* = (1−α) × I / Py
  5. Substitute back to get:   Qx* = α × I / Px
The Demand Functions:
Qx* = (α × I) / Px
Qy* = ((1−α) × I) / Py
With current values (α = 0.60, I = $60):
Qx* = (0.60 × $60) / $2.00 = 18.0
Qy* = (0.40 × $60) / $3.00 = 8.0
Key Insight: The consumer spends exactly α = 60% of income on X and (1−α) = 40% on Y, regardless of prices. This is a unique property of Cobb-Douglas preferences.

Five Key Takeaways from Cobb-Douglas Optimization:
  1. Consumer spends 60% on X and 40% on Y — set by α alone
  2. Doubling Px cuts Qx in half, but spending on X is unchanged
  3. Doubling income doubles both quantities (unit income elasticity)
  4. At the optimum: MRS = Px/Py (indifference curve tangent to budget line)
  5. At the optimum: MUx/Px = MUy/Py (equal marginal utility per dollar)
